Introduction

In modern automotive and industrial electronic systems, DC-DC converters have become increasingly important due to the rising demand for high efficiency and reliable operation across a wide load and environmental conditions. Traditional analog-controlled converters often struggle to balance fast transient response, stability, and digital integration requirements in advanced CMOS processes. These challenges motivate the design of a digitally assisted power converter that can achieve both high performance and high robustness.

This project aims to design a digital PID-controlled CMOS DC-DC buck converter featuring zero-crossing detection (ZCD) for enhanced switching efficiency (in DCM). The objective is to achieve stable regulation across a wide output range while minimizing switching loss, improving transient response, and enabling seamless integration with digital system-on-chip architectures. By leveraging a digital PID controller, the converter benefits from programmability, PVT tolerance, and predictable loop dynamics compared with traditional analog control methods.

System Architecture

Blocks designed in the proposed converter include flash ADC, power FETs, gate drivers, non-overlap dead-time generation, DCVS level shifters, a PID controller, a digital PWM waveform generator, a zero-crossing detection (ZCD) module, scan chain logic, and clock generation blocks. The LCR output stage is implemented off-chip due to its large passive component requirements.

Main Components

The converter employs a flash ADC architecture to provide the high-speed conversion needed for fast control loops. Because a buck converter does not require high resolution, a 3-bit ADC was selected to balance performance, area, and complexity, resulting in a reasonable number of comparators. Each comparator uses a strong-arm latch topology, chosen for its high speed and low power consumption.

To further reduce comparison delay and input-referred offset, the input differential pair is upsized to improve transconductance and mitigate device mismatch. The remaining transistors in the latch are kept smaller than the input-pair to minimize parasitic capacitances, thereby lowering delay. Additionally, decoupling capacitors can be introduced to reduce clock feedthrough and kickback noise, improving overall comparator accuracy.
